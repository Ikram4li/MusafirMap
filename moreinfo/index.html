The Fiqh (Islamic Law) 

Distance Ruling

In classical fiqh, the journey was not defined by kilometers or miles. Instead, it was measured in time taken to travel: typically three days and nights of travel by camel or foot.

Why the Difference of Opinion

All four madhÄhib agree that a minimum distance threshold exists. The minimum distance someone must travel before being considered a traveller is four baarids (equivalent to four farsakhs). Later scholars converted these travel durations into modern distance estimates, which generally fall between 77â€“88 km (48â€“54.5 miles).

However, older measurement systemsâ€”such as Roman and Scandinavian milesâ€”were not identical, so when scholars translated the classical distances into modern units, slight differences emerged. This is the main reason contemporary estimates vary.

In addition to the distance requirement, classical jurists also emphasised that a person must leave the limits of their city or town (the built-up area) before the rulings of travel apply. Simply travelling a long distance within a large metropolitan area does not constitute being a musÄfir.

So, within each madhab, the concept is the same, but the exact number in km/miles is a modern approximation, not an intrinsic fiqh disagreement.

As the crow flies vs distance travelled

A common question today is whether the minimum travel distance should be measured as the crow flies (straight-line distance) or according to the actual route travelled.

Classical scholars understood travel distance in terms of the actual journey undertaken, not theoretical straight-line measurements. However, with modern infrastructure  such as : roads, motorways, rail networks, and more direct travel routes, the difference between "as the crow flies distance" and actual travelled distance is often much smaller than it was historically.

MusafirMap uses a radius (straight-line) distance around the selected home city. Once a user crosses this boundary (Traveller's Threshold ğŸŸ¦), they have exceeded the minimum linear distance that fulfills the requirement according to the more conservative interpretation of distance.

Users who prefer to follow the distance-travelled method can still use MusafirMap to identify where their city limits end (Home City Boundary â¬›) and then track the actual miles or kilometres of their route from that point onward.

This approach provides flexibility while maintaining fidelity to the core fiqh principle: that the ruling applies when a person genuinely leaves their home city and travels a significant distance.

Divine Words on Travel

The Qur'an itself hints at these concessions. Allah says, "And when you travel throughout the land, there is no blame upon you for shortening the prayer..." (An-Nisa 4:100). This verse is a foundational stone for understanding the permissibility of Qasr.

Furthermore, the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) exemplified these rulings. His actions and sayings provide the practical application and context for the Qur'anic verses, guiding us on how to implement these divine mercies.

Consider the hadith where Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) reported that prayer was shortened during travel. This historical account solidifies the practice and its acceptance within the Muslim community.
